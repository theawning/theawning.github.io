The Awning

On Aberrator

An introduction by Awn

Have you been subjected to an icebreaker recently? As a doctoral student who did not take take a break after their undergraduate program, these have been imposed on me for many years. Typically the format involves the sharing of some superficial traits such as the names, fields of study, and preferred pronouns of the unwitting participants. Sometimes other information is requested. This can range from practical (e.g. “where are you from?”, “what year in the program are you?”) to frivolous (e.g. “what is your favorite ice cream flavor?”). Occasionally an attempt is made to encourage the memorization of names, for example by repeating all the names and factoids of earlier participants, or to encourage further conversation among participants who share some traits.

When one is suggested, I often jest of the need for an ice pick instead. I don’t find icebreakers to be particularly helpful. Although I might remember some of the traits and the faces, I tend not to remember names. Taking in all the information at once only makes it harder. Also, asking for pronouns is just so gauche. I have been told that asking everyone to declare some pronouns makes for a more comfortable environment for those whose pronouns might not conform to traditional expectations and reduces the likelihood of misgendering someone. However, it seems that the process is more likely to simply isolate and identify those without traditional pronouns, unless there are many of them. Furthermore, I find it somewhat invasive to essentially demand to know someone’s gender. What if they don’t know or don’t want to share? Perhaps one day expectations of gender expression can be divorced from gender identity and this will cease to be an issue.

Anyway, I have always felt the need for a better icebreaker. A friend of mine likes to ask: What color are you feeling today? While I am amicable to the quaint sentiment of this question, it often leaves one without any real information, which is not ideal for an icebreaker. So I am partial to my own icebreaker question: What is your epithet?

For those unfamiliar with epithets, these are brief descriptors which typically follow a name, providing further information about that entity. While epithets are found aplenty among gods and kings of all types, I find the purest forms are those of the Roman cult deities. Here is a brief table with some examples:

Deity Name Epithet Translation
Jupiter Optimus Maximus the best and the greatest
Juno Moneta the warner
Mars Ultor the avenger
Sol Invictus the unconquered
Joshua Cristo the anointed

Clever readers might have noticed that Jupiter already contains an epithet within, essentially reading as “Zeus, our Father, the best and the greatest”, so this god has three epithets in one! Indeed, these deities had many epithets and were made malleable in part by the invocation of different epithets in different situations. I have slipped in the last one to emphasize the flexibility of this form. Although some might object that he was not Roman, his cult and deification certainly were. That is reflected in the use of epithets as it is with other facets of that tradition.

I would also note the epithets in my tradition. Consider the formula for any blessing, which reads, literally: “my master, our god, king of the world.” So three epithets. Or the thirteen attributes of mercy which would read as a list of epithets to anyone in the ancient Mediterranean world. In fact, people in the polytheistic world of those times would have seen the many epithets attributed to the monotheistic god as a symbol of supremacy over human kings and false idols alike.

The malleability of epithets is what makes a declaration of epithets so useful for an icebreaker. The epithet can vary as needed. It can be sincere to provoke conversation or comical as a means of deflection. If you are feeling cheeky, just introduce yourself as Optimus Maximus. After all, we were created in the image of god, so why not?

In my table, I have tried to pick what I feel is the most fundamental epithet, the most essential one, the one which is most strongly associated with the character. I think that is an important thing to understand. So as a means of introduction, I would like to inform you that my epithet is: Aberrator.

To aberrate is to wander, so an aberrator is a wanderer. The word has Latin roots ab, meaning ‘from’ or ‘away from’, and erro, meaning ‘stray’. It is often pejorative, having the same root as the word ‘error’. I first encountered the word ‘aberration’ when studying electron microscopy as an undergraduate. This use of the word as a technical term, to refer to distortions in an optical system, is very much pejorative. Aberrations are important to me as I have chosen to research in the field of electron microscopy as a graduate student. Those who require eyeglasses may be familiar with aberrations in the technical sense as well. For those who are not, I will try to provide a brief introduction.

Aberrations can be described in order of increasing complexity. The most basic aberration is defocus, which manifests as an anisotropic blurring of the image. Anyone who has used a camera is likely familiar with this effect. In the case that you somehow have not, defocus may be simulated by placing an object close to your eyes, looking at that object to focus on it, and observing some objects more distant. If your focus is maintained on the nearby object, the distant objects should appear blurred as these are beyond the depth of focus of human eyes. An ideal object with which to observe this phenomenon is a large piece of paper with text or fabric with a pattern throughout, where the blurring effect on distant text or motifs would be readily apparent when focusing on nearby details. The next is two-fold astigmatism, which manifests as a directional blurring. This can be simulated by squinting when looking at a bright light in the dark. One interesting component of two-fold astigmatism is that the direction of blurring is orthogonal at depths on either side of the focal point. Some higher order aberrations include three-fold astigmatism, coma, and spherical aberration. These are more difficult to simulate or observe offhand, though all tend to blur the image in various ways. Then there is chromatic aberration, where different wavelengths or colors in the illuminating beam are focused to different depths. Aberrations more complicated than these are typically described only mathematically and not named.

As should be clear from this description, aberrations are detrimental to traditional microscopes. In these optical systems, lenses or other optical elements magnify the illuminating beam to directly produce an image of the object. Aberrations result in the aforementioned artifacts in those images. However, aberrations may be advantageous or necessary for scanning diffraction microscopy. In this alternative mode, the illuminating beam is focused to a fine point and rastered about the object to collect diffraction patterns at each point. Then an image of the object is reconstructed from this data. The most basic way to produce an image is to use the sum of certain pixels in the diffraction patterns as the intensity of an image formed by all the scan positions. Aberrations are still detrimental to such simple images. However, it is possible to consider the images produced in this manner as images of the same object observed along different lines of sight. Therefore, it is possible to use the parallax effect, where the relative position of object features shifts to an increased degree with depth along the line of sight, to align the simple images and produce an enhanced image. A moderate amount of defocus is necessary for this algorithm to succeed and small amounts of other aberrations may be acceptable. A small amount of defocus is also beneficial for another ptychography. In this algorithm, diffraction patterns are simulated by guessing the illuminating beam shape and object features. The simulated diffraction patterns are compared to experimental ones to improve the guess for the illuminating beam and the object until the simulated diffraction patterns match the experimental patterns. It has been shown by calculation and experiment that small amounts of defocus allow for a successful reconstruction with increased scan step size, which is desirable to reduce the extent to which the object must be subjected to the beam. This is essential for sensitive objects that may be particularly susceptible to destruction by the illuminating beam. My personal speculation is that other aberrations will improve the imaging capability of ptychography in different ways which have yet to be demonstrated. One other feature of ptychography is that some depth information may be determined by simulating the propagation of the illuminating beam through a three-dimensional object. I suspect it is possible to deliberately introduce some small two-fold astigmatism to the illuminating beam in order to enhance the resolution of this depth information. As the intensity of astigmatism will increase with distance from the focal point and the direction of astigmatism will be orthogonal on either side of the focal point, this information could be incorporated into the reconstruction algorithm for increased depth resolution. Perhaps the higher order aberrations, utilized in moderation, may provide yet more information, of which I have not speculated.

As a doctoral student, I have been accused of wandering, perhaps of aberrating and certainly in a pejorative sense, in the course of my research. In fact, I suspect that this is the true reason for my recent departure from my former research group. I was too willing to try new or different things, to experiment with foreign methods, to stray from the imagined path. So why would I take this as my epithet? Well, I find this accusation absurd! What is the point of research if everything goes to plan? How can anything new be learned if one must follow an established path? If ptychography had only been attempted with perfect beams, the benefits of aberrations may never have been discovered. So, despite the potential adversity, I will keep wandering in my research.

Besides my professional life, I feel that I have been doing some physical wandering. It seems to me that, one way or another, I do not stay in one place for so long. Yet, despite my wandering, I do not go far. I was born in the San Francisco Bay Area but was moved to Orange County for elementary school. I stayed there only until the fourth grade, when I returned to the north. Then I changed locations within Northern California when I changed school systems after the seventh grade and, of course, when going to college. However, I soon returned home for the duration of the pandemic before going back to Los Angeles for graduate school. After just two years of that, I was back in the Bay again for research, then back to LA for the same. Of course, the moment I returned to LA, we had those terrible fires in the Pacific Palisades and Altadena. So I left and came back yet again. Since then I have been going back and forth from north to south for research purposes as my experiments require particular equipment. So overall, I have not stayed in one place for more than five years. The time between my general movements has steadily decreased to perhaps two. Moreover, my wandering has become ever more intense in recent months.

Perhaps I am cursed, with the first curse, to wander? Have I acquired a new mark of Cain? Then I remember that ‘my father was a wandering [lit: lost] Aramean’ and I think of my ancestors. My father’s family is from Galacia, in eastern Europe, and in the course of two generations they came to New York and spread across the United States with my grandfather ending up in California. It is much the same with my mother’s family who went from Punjab to Tanzania to New York and finally to California, also in two generations. Of course my more distant ancestors surely had their own paths to Punjab and Galacia from Eretz Israel. Still, was all of that wandering? I am not so sure. It is not quite wandering to flee from one place to another, as my ancestors were largely doing. Certainly there is a difference also between traveling or visiting and wandering. In my movements, I have gone from one place to another, hoping to stay but choosing to move yet again. Most of all, I have gone back and forth, which feels more like wandering than fleeing or escaping.

In all my wandering, I have often felt like an outsider or guest. I certainly have a lot of experience establishing myself and making new friends in a new place. It is a far cry from the modern American idyll of a small yet stalwart hometown, from which one might emerge only upon their enrollment in a college or university. Or from life in an ancient village, which residents may never leave. Perhaps this is the new normal, especially for young people such as myself in a world perhaps more mobile and less stable than ever. My family is well practiced in playing the part of the wandering Jew. For that matter it is fortunate that I am Jewish for I know that I will always have a home, a place to belong, among fellow Jews, wherever I go. This phenomenon of wandering has been a core feature of the Jewish experience over the years and I have found it in my life as well. I can only hope that given recent events, I will not be forced to become a wandering Jew like those of times past.

So what is your epithet? You can see now why I consider myself Aberrator. Maybe is true that not all those who wander . . .
